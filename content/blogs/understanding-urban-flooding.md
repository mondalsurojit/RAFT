Urban flooding is one of the most visible — and fastest growing — climate risks facing Indian cities. This note walks through why it happens, what makes it hard to predict, and how a flood information system pulls the pieces together.

## What makes a city flood

A flood is, at its simplest, more water arriving at a place than can drain away from it. In a city, three things tilt that balance:

- **Impervious surfaces.** Concrete and asphalt stop rain from soaking into the ground, so almost all of it becomes runoff.
- **Drainage capacity.** Storm drains are sized for a design storm. When rainfall is more intense than that design, the excess has nowhere to go.
- **Local topography.** Water collects in low-lying pockets — underpasses, junctions and old lake beds — long after the rain has stopped.

When an intense burst of rain meets a saturated, paved catchment, the result is the familiar waterlogging that brings a city to a halt.

## Why it is hard to forecast

Predicting an urban flood is harder than predicting the rain that causes it. The rainfall has to be forecast accurately in both **space** and **time** — a storm cell a few kilometres off, or an hour early, changes which neighbourhoods are affected.

That rainfall then has to be translated into water depth on the ground, which depends on the drainage network, the soil, and what happened in the previous hours. Small errors compound at every step.

### The role of data

Good forecasts start with good observations. Rain gauges, weather radar, satellite rainfall estimates and even reports from residents each fill a gap the others miss. Combining them gives a fuller picture than any single source can.

## From data to decisions

A flood information system turns those observations into something a person can act on. Broadly, it moves through four stages:

1. **Collect** flood-relevant data from many sources.
2. **Analyse** it to understand how water is moving through the catchment.
3. **Model and forecast** rainfall, depth and extent.
4. **Disseminate** clear, location-specific information to the people who need it.

The goal is not a perfect prediction — it is a useful one, delivered early enough to matter.
